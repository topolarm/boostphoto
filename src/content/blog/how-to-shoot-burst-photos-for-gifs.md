Not every burst sequence makes a good GIF. I learned this the hard way — after stitching together 25 frames of a first dance where my camera was bouncing all over the place, the result looked less "romantic moment" and more "found footage horror film."

The difference between a burst that becomes a beautiful animation and one that becomes a jittery mess comes down to how you shoot it. Camera settings, body position, timing, and knowing which moments are worth a burst in the first place.

Here's everything I've learned from making hundreds of burst-to-GIF animations across 200+ weddings.

## Camera Settings for Burst Photography

### Continuous shooting mode

This one's obvious, but let's be specific. Set your camera to **continuous high-speed shooting** — not single shot, not continuous low. You want the fastest frame rate your camera offers.

Most modern cameras do 10-20fps in continuous mode. Some go much higher: the Sony A9 III hits 120fps in electronic shutter mode. The Canon R3 does 30fps. Even entry-level mirrorless bodies typically manage 8-10fps.

**The minimum for smooth GIFs is about 5fps.** Below that, the gaps between frames are too large and the animation will look choppy. The sweet spot is **10fps or higher** — you get fluid motion without needing an extreme number of frames.

Burst2GIF supports playback from 5fps all the way to 120fps, so whatever your camera produces, the plugin can handle it.

### Autofocus: AF-C (Continuous)

For moving subjects — which is most burst-worthy moments — set your autofocus to **continuous** (AF-C on Nikon/Sony, AI Servo on Canon). This keeps the camera tracking focus between frames.

If your subject is relatively still (say, a couple standing during ring exchange), single AF works fine. But for anything with real movement — an aisle walk, a dance spin, confetti throw — continuous AF prevents the later frames from going soft.

**Eye-AF** is a game-changer here if your camera supports it. Lock onto the subject's eye and let the camera track through the entire burst. The result: every frame is tack-sharp, which makes the final GIF dramatically better.

### Shutter speed

This depends on what you're shooting, but a general rule for burst-to-GIF work:

**1/250s or faster for action moments** (confetti, bouquet toss, dancing). This freezes each frame cleanly. When frames are played back as a GIF, frozen motion between frames creates that satisfying stop-motion-to-smooth effect.

**1/125s for slower moments** (aisle walk, couple portraits with gentle movement). A tiny bit of motion blur in each frame actually makes the animation smoother, similar to how video at 24fps uses motion blur to feel cinematic.

Avoid going below 1/60s for handheld burst shooting. Camera shake becomes a real problem when you're holding the shutter down for 2-3 seconds.

### RAW vs JPEG

**Both work.** Burst2GIF reads your photos through Lightroom's rendering engine, so RAW files with all your develop adjustments are fully supported.

That said, there's a practical consideration: **RAW bursts fill your buffer fast.** If your camera's buffer is 40 RAW frames but 200 JPEGs, you might want to shoot JPEG for burst-specific moments when you know the photos are destined for animation rather than print.

My approach: I shoot RAW always. The buffer on modern cameras (especially with CFexpress cards) is deep enough for 20-30 frame bursts without issues. And having RAW gives me full editing flexibility in Lightroom before creating the GIF.

### ISO and exposure

Nothing special here — expose as you normally would. The only thing to watch is **consistency across the burst**. If you're shooting in Manual mode, every frame will have identical exposure, which makes for a cleaner animation.

In Aperture Priority or Shutter Priority, the camera might shift exposure slightly between frames if the light changes (like a flash firing during the burst, or moving from shade to sun). This creates a "flicker" effect in the final GIF.

**For the smoothest results, shoot Manual** — especially indoors or in controlled lighting.

## 10 Best Moments for Burst GIFs at Weddings

Knowing *when* to hold the shutter is half the battle. Here are the moments I always shoot as burst sequences, and why each works well as a GIF.

### 1. Walking down the aisle

The movement is natural and continuous — steps, dress flowing, expressions changing. The emotional weight makes it one of the most impactful GIFs in any wedding gallery. Shoot a 2-3 second burst as they pass your position. 20-30 frames at 10fps gives you a perfect animation.

### 2. First look

The reaction unfolds over several seconds. The turn, the gasp, the tears. It's often slow enough that even 8fps captures plenty of detail. Position yourself to see the reactor's face, hold the shutter from just before the turn through the embrace. These GIFs are consistently the ones clients share first.

### 3. First dance

Spinning, dipping, laughing — first dances have constant movement. The classic GIF: a spin where the dress fans out. Shoot multiple bursts throughout the dance. Even 10 frames of a slow sway has a beautiful, gentle quality as a GIF.

### 4. Confetti or rice throw

Chaotic, colorful, joyful. Confetti rising and falling through the frame is mesmerizing as a photo sequence. This is also the most forgiving moment for burst GIFs — the motion is everywhere, so even imperfect framing looks great.

### 5. Bouquet toss

Action *and* reaction in a single sequence. If you can position yourself to catch both the toss and the crowd reaching, you'll have a GIF that tells a complete mini-story. The arc of the bouquet through the air is particularly satisfying in animation.

### 6. Cake cutting

Relaxed, fun, good expressions. The couple is usually laughing, there's often a playful moment with the frosting. It's a lower-energy burst — maybe 10-15 frames — but the intimacy works well as a short GIF.

### 7. Sparkler exit

Magical. The sparklers create light trails between frames that look incredible in GIF form. Shoot at a slower FPS playback (5-8fps) to let each frame breathe. The key is a slightly longer exposure per frame (1/60s-1/125s) so the sparklers have visible trails.

### 8. Couple portraits with movement

Ask the couple to walk toward you, spin, or have the partner lift and twirl the other. Directed movement gives you control over the action, and you can repeat it until you nail the burst. These GIFs often end up as the highlight of the gallery.

### 9. Ring exchange

Subtle but meaningful. The hands, the ring sliding on, the tiny smile. This works best as a slow, short GIF — 10-15 frames at 5-8fps. Keep it tight: crop to hands and faces.

### 10. Guest reactions during speeches

Candid gold. The laughing, the crying, the heckling. You can't plan these, but if you're already in burst mode pointed at the right table when the best man delivers the punchline, you'll capture a sequence that's pure joy.

## Stability: The Make-or-Break Factor

A shaky burst produces a shaky GIF. Period. This is the single most common reason burst animations look bad.

### Tripod

The gold standard for stable bursts. Your framing is locked, every pixel aligns perfectly between frames, and the only movement in the GIF is your subject. Couple portraits with directed movement are ideal tripod-burst situations.

The problem: at a wedding, you rarely have time to set up a tripod. It works for planned moments (portraits, detail shots), not candid ones.

### Handheld — how to make it work

Most wedding burst shooting is handheld. Here's how to keep it stable:

**Tuck your elbows in.** Arms out = camera shake. Elbows against your ribs creates a stable platform.

**Brace against something.** A wall, a doorframe, a pew. Any contact point between your body and a solid surface helps.

**Exhale, then shoot.** Same technique as rifle shooting. Take a breath, let half out, hold, then fire the burst. Your body is more stable between breaths.

**Use IBIS and lens stabilization.** If your camera and lens have stabilization, make sure it's on. Modern IBIS systems can compensate for significant handheld movement. Sony's Active Stabilization, Canon's IS, Nikon's VR — they all help.

**Keep bursts short.** The longer you hold the shutter, the more you'll drift. A 1.5-2 second burst (15-20 frames at 10fps) is easier to keep steady than a 4-second burst.

### Post-capture stabilization

If your burst has minor shake, software can sometimes fix it. Burst2GIF doesn't include built-in stabilization, but you can align your frames manually in Lightroom before export: sync the crop across all frames, adjusting slightly per frame to center the subject. It's tedious for large sets but effective for a few frames.

## How Many Photos Per Burst?

This question comes up constantly. Here's the breakdown:

**5-10 frames:** Very short animation. At 10fps playback, that's 0.5-1 second. Works for subtle movements — a smile forming, a head turn. Can feel abrupt.

**15-20 frames:** The sweet spot. At 10fps, you get 1.5-2 seconds of animation. Long enough to show real motion, short enough to keep file size reasonable. This is what I aim for with most wedding bursts.

**25-30 frames:** Extended animation. Good for longer actions like a full aisle walk or a dance sequence. At 10fps, that's 2.5-3 seconds. Still manageable.

**40+ frames:** Getting long. The GIF or MP4 file size increases, and the animation might feel like it should just be a video at that point. Only go this long for something truly continuous, like a sparkler exit walk.

My rule: **hold the shutter for about 2 seconds.** At 10fps, that gives me ~20 frames. Enough for a smooth, satisfying animation without excess.

## Editing: Sync Your Lightroom Settings

Once your burst photos are in Lightroom, you'll want them to look consistent before making the GIF. A burst where frame 1 is warm and frame 15 is cool creates a jarring flicker in the animation.

### The fast way:

1. Edit the first frame of the burst — adjust exposure, white balance, tone curve, crop, everything
2. Select all frames in the burst (click first, Shift+click last)
3. Click **Sync Settings** (or Ctrl/Cmd+Shift+S)
4. Check all the boxes (or "Check All")
5. Hit Synchronize

Now all frames match. This takes about 30 seconds per burst.

One thing to watch: if your crop varies between frames (maybe you want to center the subject slightly differently), sync everything *except* crop, then adjust individual frames.

This sync step is what makes the Lightroom → Burst2GIF workflow so powerful. Edit once, sync, export to GIF. Your entire photo sequence has consistent color, exposure, and style.

## Slow GIF vs Fast GIF: Choosing Your FPS

The playback FPS you set in your GIF tool completely changes the feel of the animation.

### Slow (5-8 FPS)

Each frame is visible for a noticeable moment. The effect is artistic, almost like a slideshow with very fast transitions. This works for:

- Emotional moments where you want each expression to land (first look reactions)
- Sparkler exits where you want to appreciate the light trails
- Sequences with only 5-10 frames (playing slow makes them last longer)
- An intentional "stop-motion" aesthetic

### Medium (10-12 FPS)

Natural-looking motion without feeling like a video. This is the default I use for 90% of wedding GIFs. The movement is smooth but you can still perceive individual frames if you look closely. It hits the sweet spot between "animated photos" and "video clip."

### Fast (15-30 FPS)

Approaching real video smoothness. At 24fps, you're essentially at cinematic frame rate. Use this when:

- You have 30+ frames from a high-speed burst
- The moment was very fast (confetti throw at 20fps camera = 40 frames in 2 seconds)
- You want the animation to feel like a video clip rather than animated photos
- Your camera shoots at 20+fps and you captured enough frames

### Ultra-fast (30-120 FPS)

Only relevant if you're shooting with cameras like the Sony A9 III at 120fps. At these capture rates, you can create actual slow-motion animations — play back 120 captured frames at 30fps, and you get a 4x slow-motion GIF. Stunning for fast action like confetti or water splashes.

## Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

### Too few frames

Shooting 5 frames and expecting a smooth GIF will leave you disappointed. That's a half-second animation at 10fps. Always aim for at least 15 frames.

### Too much camera movement

This is the #1 killer. If your camera moves more than your subject, the GIF will be unwatchable. Use the stability techniques above, and keep your bursts under 2 seconds when handheld.

### Bad timing

Starting the burst too late means you miss the peak moment. For planned moments (bouquet toss, confetti), start your burst *just before* the action begins. Better to have 5 "boring" frames at the start than to miss the money frames.

### Subject too far away

A burst of a tiny figure in a wide shot won't read as a GIF. The movement is too small to perceive at web resolution. Get closer or use a longer lens. The subject should fill at least a third of the frame for the motion to be visible.

### Inconsistent editing

If you don't sync your Lightroom settings across the burst, you'll get exposure or color flicker in the final animation. Always sync before exporting.

### Forgetting to shoot in burst mode

It sounds silly, but after switching to single shot for detail photos, it's easy to forget to switch back. I've missed more than one great burst moment because my camera was on single-frame. Build a habit: check your drive mode before key moments.

## Putting It Together

The workflow, start to finish:

1. Set your camera to continuous high-speed, AF-C, Manual exposure, shutter speed 1/250s+
2. Identify the moment — aisle walk, first dance, confetti, whatever the action is
3. Brace yourself, tuck your elbows, find a contact point if possible
4. Start the burst just before the peak action, hold for ~2 seconds
5. In Lightroom: edit first frame, select all burst frames, sync settings
6. Select the frames, run [Burst2GIF](/blog/burst-photos-to-gif-guide), choose your FPS and format, export
