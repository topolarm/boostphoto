When a couple opens their wedding gallery and a photo starts moving — the groom wiping away a tear, confetti frozen mid-air suddenly swirling down — that reaction is priceless. I've been delivering animated moments in my wedding galleries for over a year now, and the client feedback has been unanimous: these living photos are the highlight of the entire gallery.

But getting GIFs and MP4 videos to appear *between* your photos in the right chronological order? That's where most photographers get stuck. The good news: once you understand the workflow, it takes almost no extra time.

This guide covers the exact process for both Pic-Time and Pixieset, the two most popular gallery platforms for wedding photographers.

## Why Clients Love Animated Moments in Their Gallery

Static photos tell the story. Animated moments *relive* it.

Think about the first look — that half-second where his face changes from nervous anticipation to pure emotion. A single photo captures one frame of that. A burst GIF captures the entire transformation in 2 seconds of looping motion.

Or the bouquet toss. The reach, the grab, the celebration. A rapid-fire sequence turned into an MP4 shows the energy in a way no single frame can.

After delivering over 200 weddings, I can tell you that clients mention the animated moments in their gallery more than any single photo. One bride told me: *"The photos were absolutely touching, especially the moving images between the photos. I keep going back to them."*

The key is that these animated pieces sit right between your regular photos, in the exact moment they happened. Not dumped at the end of the gallery. Not in a separate folder. Right there in the timeline, where they belong.

## How to Shoot for Gallery GIFs

I won't go deep on shooting technique here — I've written a [complete guide to the best wedding moments for burst photos](/blog/best-wedding-moments-for-burst-photos) — but the essentials are:

**Camera settings:** Continuous burst mode (mechanical shutter preferred), 8-20 fps depending on your body. Shoot 1-3 second bursts — that's 10-30 frames, which translates to a smooth 2-3 second animation.

**What to look for:** Any moment with movement or emotion unfolding over time. First looks, veil tosses, ring exchanges, first dance dips, sparkler exits, confetti throws, bouquet tosses, cake smashing, group cheers.

**Mindset shift:** You're not looking for *the* decisive moment anymore. You're capturing *the entire moment* as it unfolds. Think of your burst as a 2-second film clip.

At a typical wedding, I shoot 15-25 burst sequences and end up delivering 10-15 animated moments in the final gallery.

The process takes about 10 seconds per animation:

1. **Select your burst photos** in Lightroom Library. These are the consecutive frames from one burst sequence — typically 10-30 images.

2. **Launch the plugin:** Library > Plug-in Extras > Burst2GIF.

3. **Set your options:** Choose your frame rate (8-12 fps works best for most wedding moments), select GIF or MP4 format, pick your output size.

But the real magic for gallery delivery is what happens next: Burst2GIF automatically imports the finished GIF or MP4 back into your Lightroom catalog. And it sets the capture time to match the original burst sequence.

That capture time is everything. It's what makes the gallery workflow possible.

## The Pic-Time Workflow in Detail

This is the workflow I use for every single wedding. It takes zero extra time beyond the 10 seconds per GIF creation, because everything happens inside Lightroom.

### Step 1: Create Your Animations in Lightroom

Go through your wedding edit. Whenever you hit a burst sequence you want to animate, select the frames and run Burst2GIF. The plugin exports the GIF/MP4 and imports it back into your Lightroom catalog automatically.

After processing all your burst sequences, you'll have your animated files sitting right there in your Lightroom catalog, with capture timestamps that place them in the correct chronological position among your photos.

### Step 2: Export Everything Together

When you're ready to export for delivery, select your entire wedding collection — photos AND the animated files — and export. In Lightroom's export dialog, make sure **"Include Video Files"** is checked. This tells Lightroom to include your MP4/GIF files alongside the photos.

Export everything to one folder. Your photos and animations end up together, and the filenames reflect the chronological order because Burst2GIF set the correct capture times.

### Step 3: Upload to Pic-Time

Open your Pic-Time gallery and upload the entire folder. Since Pic-Time reads the file metadata (including capture time), your animated files slot into the correct position between your photos automatically.

The result: your client scrolls through their gallery, and every few photos, a moment comes alive. The first look loops. The confetti falls. The sparklers wave. All in the right place in the timeline.

### Important Pic-Time Details

**File size limit:** Pic-Time accepts GIF uploads up to approximately 50MB. For most burst sequences at web resolution (1500-2000px wide), a 20-30 frame GIF will be 15-30MB — well within the limit.

**MP4 is preferred.** The same 30-frame sequence that produces a 20MB GIF will be just 1-3MB as MP4. Better quality, full color depth, dramatically smaller files. Pic-Time plays both formats inline, so there's no reason to stick with GIF unless you have a specific need for it.

**Auto-play behavior:** On Pic-Time, GIFs auto-play as the client scrolls. MP4s display with a small play indicator but play inline — the client stays in the gallery view. Both formats work beautifully in the gallery experience.

## The Pixieset Workflow

Pixieset supports a similar approach, with a few differences in how files are handled.

### Upload Process

Pixieset lets you upload photos and videos to the same gallery collection. After creating your animations with Burst2GIF and exporting from Lightroom (with video files included), upload everything to your Pixieset collection.

Pixieset will recognize the video files and display them inline among your photos. The sorting works by filename or capture date, so the Burst2GIF capture time setting keeps everything in order.

### Pixieset-Specific Tips

**Video format support:** Pixieset handles MP4 files well. GIF support is more limited in terms of file size, so MP4 is the safer choice here.

**Gallery layout:** In Pixieset's grid view, video thumbnails show a small play icon. When clicked, they play inline without leaving the gallery. The experience is smooth and professional.

**Download behavior:** When clients download their gallery, video files are included alongside photos. This means your clients get their animated moments in their download — no separate delivery needed.

## Best Practices for Gallery Animations

After delivering hundreds of galleries with animated moments, here are the patterns that work best:

### Scatter, Don't Clump

Distribute your GIFs and MP4s throughout the gallery timeline. If you shot 15 burst sequences at a wedding, they should appear naturally through the day — a few during getting ready, one or two at the ceremony, several during portraits, a couple at the reception.

The worst thing you can do is dump all animations at the end of the gallery. They lose their context and impact.

### Match the Photo Sequence

Name your files to maintain chronological order. Burst2GIF handles this automatically by setting the capture time to the original burst timestamp. When you export from Lightroom sorted by capture time, everything falls into place.

### Keep File Sizes Reasonable

For Pic-Time, stay under 50MB per GIF file. But honestly, if your GIF is approaching 50MB, switch to MP4. A 50MB GIF will be about 3-5MB as an MP4 with better quality. There's no practical reason to deliver a 50MB GIF when a 3MB MP4 looks better.

My standard settings: MP4 format, 1500px wide, 10 fps, 20-30 frames. Each file is typically 1-3MB.

### Quality Over Quantity

Not every burst needs to become an animation. Select the sequences where the movement tells a story. A burst of someone standing still with a slight head turn? Skip it. A burst of the flower girl spinning with petals flying? That's the one.

I aim for 10-15 animated moments per wedding. Enough to surprise and delight throughout the gallery, not so many that they lose their special quality.

### Test Before Delivery

Before uploading to your client gallery, preview your animations. Check for:
- Smooth looping (the start and end frames should flow naturally)
- Correct speed (too fast looks frantic, too slow feels sluggish)
- File size (especially for GIF format)
- Correct placement in the timeline
